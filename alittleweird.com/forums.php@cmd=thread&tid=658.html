Subliminal
I don't believe it. It talks about things like expression and scars and wrinkles. How can gut feelings come from that? It makes it sound like all humans express things openly. No. Wrong. If you see someone smiling, does that make you like them? Does it make them a good person? Does it really mean they're happy? If someone has a scar, does that make them bad? Or does it make them seem vulnerable, easier to like because something about them screams "I've had a hard life"?

A person's expression ultimately tells you nothing about them. It might give you a FEELING from the person, like wow they look nice or something. But not a PERCEPTION of the person, and THAT is a gut feeling. You won't look at someone laughing and smiling with their friends that you've never met before and the first thought that pops into your head is "wow, she is a bitch"-that is perception. Find a better word if you like. That is a gut feeling. Going by expression alone is misleading.

Now I'm just rambling. Okay simplify my babbling.

Expression=surface deep
Gut feeling=beneath the surface

"Â­Providing more immediate information are the changes in a person's face, such as smiles, frowns or scowls. These changes provide us with the most obvious information about someone's mood or immediate intentions. Expressions represent the person's intended message, the one he or she is trying to convey. A person trying to gain your trust will smile. Someone trying to scare you will scowl."

This is obviously written by someone who takes what they see at face-value, instead of trying to look beneath the surface or trying to think outside the box.

Here are a few basic reasons why this is false.

1) Author is assuming that people are aware of their facial expressions all the time.

-Wrong. I've lost count of the amount of times that people have said to me "You look mad. What's wrong?", when I'm not mad at all. It always catches me off-guard. People are not always aware of what their face is doing. They may subconciously be scowling, grinning, frowning; anything. This could mean that they're dwelling on some internal thought that has nothing to do with what's going on around them, or just a completely misplaced expression; the possibilities are endless. You get the gist of it. Which brings me to number two.

2) Author is assuming that the facial expression is a precise representation of what said person is thinking.

Obviously, this is not true. For one, the person's facial expression-as previously stated-may be more directed to an internal thought, or they are unaware of what their face is doing. It may also be a front. If you're one of those warm-hearted people who try to avoid hurting feelings, think of this: It's Christmas. Your aunt just bought you the most dreadful looking sweater you've ever laid eyes on. You control your expression so it doesn't reflect the horror and revulsion you feel. She is sitting across from you looking at you expectantly. You hitch up a smile and give a pleased exlamation at your gift-but inside you're screaming "GOD THIS IS HIDEOUS!".

...I don't really think I need to bore you guys any more. You probably get it.
