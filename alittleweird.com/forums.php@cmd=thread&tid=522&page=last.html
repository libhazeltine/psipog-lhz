ive fooled with foil before and have used a similar shape, however i would not call the shape "crucial." as far as to your point of friction, id say its important, so i can agree there.

ive used actual tin(aluminum) foil as well as foil wrappers from little chocolates. the best results ive had are when the point(s) of contact create as little surface area as possible. usually i will just fold a rectangular piece lengthwise and then crinkle it up in the center a bit so that it has one, or possibly two, points of contact with my desk, both of which are quite small (a crease in the tinfoil creating a point).

this design usually does not stand upright on its point(s) so that it can freely rotate without dragging, however this adds to the curiosity of the design. starting out with this i generally get a nice back and forth wobble, as it tries to find an axis on which to spin, and an orientation that provides low friction. so as a warmup it is self sufficient in that you can start by wobbling it and seeing if you can get lateral movement.

once the wobbles help it find an axis, it can spin from there... generally flopping to a non-axis position if concentration/spin is lost. i suppose this could be considered a "harder" way to do it, but i find it easier in that you dont have to worry too much about the design, and it gives you nice feedback even if its not spinning.
