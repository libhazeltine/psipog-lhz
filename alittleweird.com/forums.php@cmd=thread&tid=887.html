I would like to present something I was taught at my university if I could, that definitely supports the proposed theory in many ways.

It is the "Sapir-Whorf" hypothesis; a key point of linguistic relativity. It, very simply put, states that words will create one's reality (e.g. if you say you are stupid, you will be much more inclined to act in stupid ways). In what ways they create one's reality, or if they do at all, is highly debated. Although I'm going to present it to you as it was presented to me in my course, it would be wise to do your own research on the topic. I wouldn't suggest Wikipedia, but rather scholarly, peer-reviewed articles for your research. Trust me guys, you will see a difference O_O

I'll start out with a study performed on a group of kindergarten students. The teachers and parents were instructed not to teach the word "Bored" to the children and not to use it around them ever. Being that the word didn't exist to those children, they never got bored. My professor stated that rather than just sitting down and saying "I'm bored", they went and found something to do. At the end of the study, the teachers and parents were instructed to explain the word "Bored" to the children, and thus after the children started getting lazier and even sat and said "I'm bored" rather than finding something to do like they used to. They didn't know the word bored, so they didn't know the concept of boredom and furthermore they didn't get bored (until the concept was learned)!

Linguistic relativity, as it relates to behavioral sciences and psychology, often states that one creates their identity with what words they use to describe themselves, especially to their peers or primary social group. In your case, telling yourself (and others about yourself) that you have a very negative point of view on everything (like you used to) would make you much more inclined to have a negative point of view on everything, while telling yourself "I have a very positive view on everything" would have the opposite effect. In the children's cases, their immediate reality was effected just by not knowing, then learning, the word "bored".

Linguistic relativity is similar to the theory of thoughts creating one's reality (cognitive relativity?), however due to thoughts being subjective, it is even more difficult to test than linguistic relativity is. However, words are projected thoughts, so in non-materialistic way, this would support some theories stating that thoughts create one's reality, which is primarily what that "The Secret" book tries to get it, which is a derivative of or from your "Law of Attraction" theory there :)
