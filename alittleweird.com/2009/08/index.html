August 28th, 2009 by Sean“Virtual Out of Body Experiences”

http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4PQAc_Z2OfQ

http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-xWowv7Qryc

I applaud scientists for investigating the brain and how our vision plays a role in sensing the location of our body parts.

However.

I do not applaud their use of the term “out of body experience”.  What they are investigating is not the same thing as the traditional out of body experience.

The term “out of body experience” is broad enough to describe both phenomenon, but using it for these studies implies a relationship between the two phenomenon.  There is no relationship between the optical illusion the scientists are playing with, and the OBE state of mind that Monroe and others have investigated.

What bothers me more is that the second video uses terms like “flashes of light” and “vibrations” - however, they’re just talking about the lights and motors attached to the subjects back!  Not visions of flashing lights experienced during meditation (thought to be synapses firing in the eye-lids), or the classical vibrations people feel while having an OBE (which have no scientific explanation yet).

The blurring of terminology will create confusion.  This means that if someone is genuinely curious about OBEs, they might read scientific articles about this optical illusion, and mistakenly conclude that OBEs are an optical illusion - for no logical reason… just because the terminology is the same.

OBEs might be an illusion - I’m ok with that.  But let that discovery come from investigation of OBEs!

Optical illusions are cool - I’m ok with that too.  But let’s stop calling this line of work “out of body experiences” because we already have a separate collection of phenomenon that already uses that term.

August 15th, 2009 by SeanNeural Networks pt. 1

Background

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a really cool subject.  It combines two things I love - programming, and the mind.  It’s interesting to read about psychology and biology… but for me, programming is more real.  A psychologist or biologist studies the mind/brain.  An AI programmer attempts to create a mind/brain.

In AI, a popular technique is to use something called a Neural Network.  Basically, the programmer first attempts to write code to mimic a single neuron.  Then, the programmer creates a network of these individual neurons, and watches them interact.  This technique comes from the belief that our brain’s power comes from the billions of networked neurons inside of it.

AI programmers first came up with the idea of making a digital Neural Network from talking to biologists.  After all, if you want to build a digital brain, why not talk to people who study real brains?  Biologists taught programmers how neurons actually worked - programmers took the concept, and create a simplified digital counter-part.

There is another way to arrive at the conclusion that a Neural Network is a good idea, though.  We can arrive there just using logic and math, without any knowledge of how real brains work.  Granted, this line of reasoning only became clear in hindsight - but it explains why evolution might have naturally created the brain in the first place:

The Neural Network’s Purpose

A Neural Network is awesome at one thing - detecting relationships.

Imagine you have a series of numbers… 12, 4, 0, 4, 16, 3, 7, 6, 9, 11.  Just with that series of numbers, you have no clue about any meaning behind it.  It could be a random sequence, or some parts might be random, but other parts might have meaning.  Some formula might determine the numbers.  Or it might represent money, or time, or butterflies, or word counts, etc.  As it stands right now - you simply don’t know.

Now imagine I feed you a stream of numbers, like so:

1. 12, 4, 0, 4, 16, 3, 7, 6, 9, 11

2. 13, 6, 0, 3, 18, 2, 7, 4, 10, 12

3. 14, 8, 0, 2, 16, 1, 7, 2, 11, 13

4. 15, 10, 0, 1, 10, 0, 7, 0, 12, 14

If you’re clever, you’ll notice there are a lot of patterns in that stream.  The first number always increases by one, the second increases by two, the third is always zero, the fourth decreases by one, the fifth is the second number times the fourth number, etc.

You can see the pattern because you have a brain, and I just told you what the patterns are.  Imagine you are a computer, with zero knowledge, and your job is to figure out the pattern from scratch.  How would you go about it?  How would you conclude that the fifth number is the second times the fourth?

Since you have no prior knowledge, what you have to do is try every combination.  Is the first number the second times the third?  No.  Is the first number the second times the fourth?  No.  Is the first number the second times the fifth?  No.  Eventually, while trying everything out, you find that the fifth number is the second times the fourth.

The only way the computer can detect a pattern is by trying every possible combination, and see if it finds anything.  In fact, this is what humans do too - if you look at a sequence of numbers, and attempt to find a pattern, you’ll sit there and try different things out… searching for something that makes sense.

Creating the Network

Why create a network of neurons?  You can think of a neuron as a little detector.  It takes in input, and spits out a “yes” or “no”.  Then, more neurons take in a collection of yes’s and no’s, and spit out their own yes or no.  So there’s a bunch of these yes’s and no’s flying around.

The part that makes this work is that a neuron learns which neighboring neurons it should listen to, and which ones it should ignore. You can think of the neuron as being networked with the neighbors it listens to.  Likewise, there are neighbors that listen to it.  This creates a web of neurons.

Network Complexity

Back to our random number example - you need a neuron at every spot you want to detect a relationship.  Is the first number the second times the third?  Stick a neuron there.  You’ll need a network of neurons capable of performing multiplication, but you’ll need one neuron at the top that fires off a “yes” or “no” if the first number is the second times the third.

We have a lot of different relationships we want to test… so we’ll need a lot of neurons.  And what if we want to test really complicated relationships?  For example, maybe we want to test the fifth number is the second, plus the third, times the sixth.  We’ll need a neuron for that.

So, the more complicated the relationships, the more neurons we’ll need.  Really, there’s no limit… because there could be an insanely complicated relationship behind two numbers.  If we don’t have enough neurons to detect the relationship, then our neural network won’t be able to figure it out.

Conclusion

There is a lot of information on Neural Networks, and I’ve glossed over a lot of the details.  The important point to recognize is that you can use a network of neurons in order to detect relationships.  The more neurons, the more complicated the relationships it can detect.  Each individual neuron performs a simple task, but when networked intelligently (from learning over time which neighbors to listen to, and which to ignore), the network is capable of extracting meaning from a stream of input.

August 8th, 2009 by SeanOBE + Lucid + Monroe

I’ve been focusing on OBEs lately.  I have equipment set up in my room, but I haven’t been able to get out of my body in order to experiment with it.  Instead, I’ve been having pretty amazing/insane lucid dreams.  Last night I slept about 10 hours, and just about all of it was lucid… it was so much fun :-).

Normally, it’s hard to remember dreams because they’re just blurry and fleeting.  Lately, my problem has been the opposite - I have so many unique and highly detailed experiences, that it’s too much to remember everything.  Waking life is incredibly repetitive, so it’s very easy to remember weeks of information, but dreams are individually unique moment to moment - one night can feel like the equivalent of five years of information.  It’s just too much :-).

I’ve also been watching OBE videos on YouTube.  One awesome channel is:

http://www.youtube.com/user/MonroeInstitute

They’ve recently posted videos of seminars with Robert Monroe.  It’s very cool to see and hear him talk.  Check it out.

I’m also still working on getting a new project up and running, dedicated to researching paranormal phenomenon.  I have a lot of ideas, and I’ve talked with other folks who have great ideas too… so now it’s a matter of trying new things out, and documenting our progress.  I don’t expect to figure everything out in a month - this is more of a long term project - but I hope it eventually turns into a useful resource.  We shall see - I’ll keep you posted.
