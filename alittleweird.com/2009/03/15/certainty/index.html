March 15th, 2009 by SeanCertainty

Why is it that, the older we get, the more certain we become about reality?

And the more certain we become, the less magic there seems to be in the world?

Of course we can answer this in the boring sense: by virtue of having “unknowns” in your belief system, you subjectively sense magic because you cannot predict the future.  It’s an inevitable result, limited to the confines of the mind.  An illusion born out of your own ignorance.

Yeah, I get it.

What if it isn’t an illusion though?  What if, by virtue of holding on to uncertainty, you genuinely affect reality, and allow magical things to happen?  Meaning that: if you hold on to uncertainty, reality itself behaves differently for you.

It’s a mind-fuck.  It blurs the line between subjectivity and objectivity.

I would like to test this theory out, but I have no clue how to go about it.  How do you measure how uncertain you are?  How do you determine whether this uncertainty is affecting reality, or mearly your perception of reality?  How do you locate and bottle up the unknown?

    The old “ruining the magic” argument? I don’t buy it.

    Think of when you were a kid and you watched kiddie shows on TV. Or your parents read you kiddie bedtime stories. Those were fun, weren’t they? But if you go back and watch/read those kiddie things now, they’ll probably seem a bit silly. Shallow. Boring. Predictable.

Or designing a database to make government just a little less wasteful and inefficient.

    While you’re growing, your brain functions essentially as a gigantic (subjective) map of all the reality outside of your body. (In some cases, it becomes a literal map: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippocampus#Role_in_spatial_memory_and_navigation )

    The brain adds new connections, and culls off ones it doesn’t need or use. It tries its best to become more and more accurate, and it “succeeds”, as long as the person whom the brain belongs to lives a life of routine and structure.

    It will never be entirely certain though. Suppose there’s a problem encountered in this routine. A completely unpredictable one, like a car accident, and a limb must be removed. Soon the brain will have to restructure its little version of reality around the lost limb, but for the most part, will have to make due with the part of reality it has stored away.

    If a brain is forced to constantly adapt to new environments, you might think it forms no structure whatsoever. That’s wrong, because it makes a generalization of everything it encounters, so it does end up forming a solid structure. In a sane individual, at least. Maybe that’s why there are people who are insane, they form a map of reality based on one transient thing, and when that is gone, they are left with nothing.

    This is the result of subjectivity, and we all know what that is. To me, subjectivity is a difference in ability or way of perceiving something. Because of subjectivity, we have people who make mistakes because of an incorrect assumption about something.
