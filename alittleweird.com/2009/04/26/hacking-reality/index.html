Full Circle, Injustice of Science »

April 26th, 2009 by SeanHacking Reality

I’ve really been struggling lately, trying to piece together a consistent view of reality.  With the introduction of emergence, I’ve finally understood the Atheist belief system.  It sounds a little silly, because I can look up Atheism in Wikipedia, and I should be able to just understand the idea instantly.  But I’ve discovered that I need to really immerse myself in an idea to truly understand what it’s saying - and this process can take weeks or months.  These past months I’ve been focusing on Atheism, and a few minutes ago I feel I’ve finally graduated it.

Let me start by paying respect to this belief system.  This Science+Emergence+Evolution concept is beyond revolutionary.  I hold deep respect for people who truly study, understand, and apply this belief system in their lives.  (Perhaps this “deep respect” is just a way to inflate my own ego, since I still use this belief system!)

The issue I had with Atheism is that I’ve experienced paranormal phenomenon.  The modern Atheist would argue that such experiences are delusions.  After all, if these experiences are real, then it really flies in the face of what Science and naturalism tells us.

I see that line of reasoning… and 20 minutes ago, I had no way to counter it.  I felt utterly confused; unable to reconcile my experiences with such a strong belief system.  Atheism offers so much explanation to what we experience, using reason, logic, and evidence, that it is an insurmountable beast.  In the world of Atheism vs. Theism, Atheism wins.  Sorry folks.  The personal God simply does not exist - or at least - the things we witness do not need a personal God to explain.  We can explain 99% of modern experiences using naturalistic lines of reasoning.

It’s that remaining 1% that is of interest to me, though.  The modern Atheist will simply ignore it, because it’s such an asshole.  It ruins everything.  If that 1% didn’t exist, Atheism would rein supreme.  But it does exist.  And I finally have a framework for accepting its existence.

Atheism shows us that - given a system with certain characteristics - life will naturally emerge.  Numbers self-organize, on their own.  All that’s required is a system flexible enough to sustain it.  For example, we already create digital life using computers.  It’s not as complex as life in this reality, but as time moves on and computing power increases, we will be able to create more stunning simulations.

The point here is that: Given a flexible enough system, life will naturally emerge.

So, the reality we live in is currently flexible enough to sustain life (obviously).  So, we witness life.  The atoms have formed molecules, which have organized themselves into viruses and cells, and further organized into community of cells, and multi-cellular organisms, and evolved via natural selection and other undiscovered mechanisms, until finally it’s you sitting in front of your screen, reading this sentence.

All of that.  Sitting on top of this flexible system.

Where does the paranormal fit into this?  Simple.  It’s experiences specific to this particular system.

The best example I can give is to think back to the digital life.  The programmer creates a flexible system, runs the simulation, and then life naturally emerges from it.  Now, imagine that the programmer has a bug in his code.  The life could literally find ways to hack the underlying system, and experience things that would seem “paranormal” to the digital inhabitants.  However, these “hacks” are very specific to the system.

The point here is that: The specific system that OUR life is built on might have some neat features that we are unaware of.

So… in conclusion.  The Atheist belief system is a perfect model for describing how life is created, how it just follows natural laws, and self-organizes on its own.  The Atheist model is simply true - given a system with enough flexibility, life WILL evolve.  It’s a mathematic truth.

However, in our particular instance, we aren’t really talking about any willy nilly flexible system.  We are talking about our specific system, that has life built on top of it.  The Atheist model is correct, but it still doesn’t say anything about our specific system.  It can’t.  All it says is that our particular system is an example of a system flexible enough to support life.

Just like the digital life could “tap into” the system that is beneath it, so too can we tap into the system we are built on.  Who knows what lies beneath the surface.

Atheism has solved a profound problem of thought concerning life and nature.  It has provided us with an amazing ability to explain the vast diversity of our experiences.  However, beneath the surface, there still exists the dark corner of the unknown.  Perhaps I do exist after all.

    Here is a little something I wrote a while back, I’m curious of your opinion:

    —

    “Some words for the atheists among us”

    Modern man tends to draw philosophical and metaphysical conclusions from this enormous body of knowledge that is called “physics” or nowadays usually just “science”. Because this whole body of knowledge is supposedly based on logic and deductive reasoning, the consensus is that the thus created metaphysical belief system (often called atheism) is far more valuable than any other belief system known to mankind. People ascribing to this belief system (for example Daniel Dennett or Richard Dawkins) thus defend it by saying it is based on this body of scientific knowledge, which has been shown to work perfectly (of which the computer I’m typing on right now is excellent evidence). What I will attempt to show is that this belief system is not more justified or less justified than any other belief system, hereby undermining the superior credibility that is often given to it just because it is based on science.

    My first argument is to show that science as a whole is incomplete by its very nature, hereby specifically referring to physics. This is done because one of the most powerful arguments for atheism called determinism (from which often the non-existence of free will follows) comes directly from results in physics. Using mainly Newtonian physics, scientists are able to calculate with a high degree of accuracy how any given object will react when forces are applied to it or when it interacts with any other object. This gives rise to the thought that if only we knew the position of and the forces acting upon each atom in the universe, we could calculate exactly what will happen in the future (or equally well what has happened in the past). If that were the case, every event in the universe would be determined in advance, because atoms can’t do anything but obey the laws of nature (hence the name determinism). However, this argument is flawed, because physics is incomplete, it can’t explain everything (as any theoretical physicist can confirm). To accept a philosophical conclusion as far reaching as determinism from an incomplete body of knowledge is in itself quite foolish, however in this case it is even worse because science can never be complete by its very nature! This is illustrated by the fact that at the end of the 19th century scientists like Lord Kelvin advised against studying theoretical physics, because they believed it was almost complete and there wouldn’t be any work left for new students. Indeed, physics at that time could explain and predict every known phenomenon, with the exception of blackbody radiation and the photoelectric effect. Now take some time to stop and think. What if these two phenomena had never been discovered? Physics would indeed be complete in the eyes of scientists back then, as everything could be explained! My point here is obvious: you can never know if science is complete or not, so how can you base something so profound as your worldview on it? There can always be some undiscovered phenomenon that could turn around all of science, invalidating the entire previous view.

    My second argument goes against the view that science is based on strict logic and deductive reasoning. Thomas Kuhn has already shown in his main work The Structure of Scientific Revolution how social and cultural aspects (rather than only logic and reasoning) play an important role in the development and acceptance of a scientific theory. I would like to point out how many bits of knowledge that ended up in mainstream science didn’t get there because of logic and deductive reasoning, but because of belief and abductive (also inductive) reasoning. The most obvious example is the currently popular theory of dark matter. It was calculated (among other reasons) out of the rotation of galaxies that the matter we observe accounts for only 4% of the measured gravitational force. According to these calculations there should be a lot more matter than is observed, so scientists invented “dark matter”. Simply said this is a kind of invisible matter, that can only be detected trough it’s gravitational effect (this isn’t the whole story of it, but the exact scientific details are irrelevant for my argument). What happens here is known as abductive reasoning, which is not logically correct: you observe something, then you invent a hypothesis that can explain that something, and then accept that hypothesis as true BECAUSE it can explain that something. Though dark matter is even by many scientists today still regarded as a working hypothesis, other theories are being built upon it already, so it might be eventually accepted and embedded in mainstream thinking.

    Besides flawed reasoning, personal beliefs of the scientists come into the picture. An example of this comes from contemporary quantum physics. What is observed in quantum physics is that the behavior of subatomic particles isn’t strictly deterministic anymore. You can’t have a complete description of a subatomic physical system (Heisenberg’s uncertainty relation), and neither can you predict with certainty how this system will behave (the core of quantum physics is governed by statistical laws). Now as I already stressed, this can’t be used as an argument either for or against determinism, however it is very interesting how different scientists deal with this issue. There are basically two positions, one accepts statistics at the core of reality or simply doesn’t know yet, and the other says there must be hidden variables which we haven’t discovered yet that determine the statistical behavior of the particles and therefore concludes that reality is still deterministic. Especially this last position is interesting, because the leading principle in their reasoning isn’t any discovery about how reality actually is, but their personal believes about how they think reality is. Here again the leading principle of theorizing isn’t pure logic or reason. I would like to stress that these last two examples only serve the purpose of defeating the popular view that science is a body of knowledge that has grown out of pure reason and logic. There are many other factors at work that make it grow, which offcourse doesn’t make its pragmatic value any less. This reduces current scientific thinking up to a certain extent to a bunch of theories that just happen to work, and in a way it is. It is a very interesting question that I am not able to answer up to this day, in what way science is a description of how reality actually is, or just a model that is able to predict how reality will behave. For example, is matter really made out of atoms, or is it just our way of thinking about matter as if it were made out of atoms, that makes science work? Thus is it possible to invent a model of matter that works just as good, but is logically incompatible with the atom-model?

    Setting these last questions aside, I have here attempted to show that the belief system of atheists isn’t in any way more or less probable than any other (consistent and coherent) belief system. There is no way that the scientific origin of the beliefs of many atheists makes their beliefs “better” in comparison to any other set of beliefs. Sadly this is still the attitude that many atheists have when debating their world view. It mostly comes down to them being smarter than non-atheists, and whoever isn’t an atheist must learn to think logically and read some science books because it is all “proven”. Offcourse the same goes for many fundamentalist Christians or Muslims, but their worldviews are a lot more open to relativity, simply holding for example “why should your book be true and not their book?”. The bottom line is that people in the modern world often can’t recognize beliefs anymore for what they are, thinking that many things beliefs they hold are actually facts. Many are falling into the trap of confusing their abstractions about reality with reality itself.

    I’ve followed your path almost from the beginning (it has been almost parallell to mine up to two years ago), and if you ask my opinion you are going in the wrong direction now (though you’ll probably learn a LOT from it). Science and intellectualism can never give real answers, they are way too relative, based on too many assumptions I’m not prepared to make. If you’d ask me, I would say you should stop trying to form so many opinions about reality to start seeing it for what it really is. You don’t have to put any opinions about reality in your head, because if you learn to look in the right way everything is already there. You have probably gone trough at least as many paradigm shifts as I have so you should know how profound it’s influence is. How I got out of the mess is by completely “emptying” my paradigm, pulling a Descartes so to say. I threw out everything I ever put in there myself, maybe that’s the only way (but probably not).

    Sothilde - thanks for the well thought out post. Here is a summary of your points (correct me if I’m wrong), and my response:

    1. Science is incomplete, therefore, it’s equivalent to any belief system that is incomplete.

    I agree that our modern understanding of reality is incomplete… and I will go even further to state that it will ALWAYS be incomplete (see: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del%27s_incompleteness_theorems). However, just because it’s incomplete doesn’t mean that it should be discarded completely. Resistors inside your computer use quantum tunneling to function - something the best scientific minds don’t understand. But it works, and you use it. We don’t need this illusive “100% complete” understanding of reality in order to proclaim its superiority.

    To demonstrate this, I will make an extreme example: suppose I believe that the Earth is round, and you believe that the Earth is flat. Which belief is superior? Or are they equivalent?

    2. Science doesn’t adhere to strict logic, therefore its inherently flawed.

    Since when do things have to adhere to strict logic in order to be correct? Truth is truth, whether it’s discovered through inductive or deductive reasoning, or some other means.

    3. Scientists are biased, therefore we can’t trust what they say.

    That’s why there’s peer review. If you have the opposite bias, or no bias (which is impossible), then you are free to challenge the modern scientific understanding.

    —

    Basically, I think you’ve set up impossible standards. You hold science, along with any other belief system, to these impossible standards… and they all fail equally. So you believe the belief systems are equal. But they aren’t.

    Science doesn’t claim to have 100% truth over reality. Science is just a method for protecting against illusion. Other belief systems that don’t incorporate protections against illusion will ultimately be consumed by false beliefs.

    I would write more, but I do agree with a lot of your criticisms, and I’d have to think some more too. Good stuff.
