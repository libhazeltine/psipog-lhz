ErikJDurwoodII
CCD-based cameras exhibit the broadest range of light sensitivity. CCD's are generally sensitive to about 350nm wavelength UV light down to the IR-A group of about 1400nm.

Most video and still CCD cameras have sheets of UV filters on the sensor's surface to protect it from the high density light as well as preserve the visible luminosity of the subject matter. A bit of clever hacking can remove these filters and produce a camera that is much more sensitive the the "non-visibles" around you.

In my experience, I have captured more in the "higher-than-visible" spectrum than I have in the lower ones. This coincides with the evidence that suggests that detectable psionic effects are in the upper regions of the EM spectrum and beyond; and it is through "collapsing down" to the lower ranges that we can observe the interaction.

see Geiger Counter experiment.
