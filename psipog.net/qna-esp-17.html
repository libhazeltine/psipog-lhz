Rainsong replies on October 17, 2004, 1:08pm:

It is _possible_ to have developed such shielding. It is, however, rather unusual to have done so. Some Empaths do indeed enjoy the sensory overload of contact with large crowds. Some even choose to engage in the so-called "feeding" on the mess of psi-stuff produced by such crowds.

If you're in a large crowd, "knowing" what it feels does not require any kind of psychic empathy. It doesn't even require psychological empathy. _If_ such walls are there, then taking them down again should open the person up to such perceptions. Of the people who have asked me about this, the vast majority had no such walls, nor any kind of unusual level of empathy, walls or no. There are perhaps half a dozen who actually had such things; again, of those who have asked me, personally.

Trained folks can --and often do-- have as much trouble as untrained folks. The difference is in "what kind of trouble", "what they choose to do about it", and "whether they recognise what the problem is". Among untrained folks, it _seems_ that the more common problem is picking up other people's psychiatric problems, and attempting suicide as a result.

Such walls as I _have_ seen are generally dismantled by a third party, in the same way that any other kind of shield would be dismantled by a third party. Not much difference from other kinds of shields, after all. Some of the "walls" are inclined to re-build themselves.

full_eclipse asks on October 17, 2004, 11:37am:

hello everyone, my question is on telepathy. ok here it goes, when Im playing guess the shape and color in your practice room i get about 88% correct on my answers, but my question is how do you know that what your receiving is the actual thought of the other person or just your immagination?

thank you for reading.

Rainsong replies on October 17, 2004, 12:54pm:

If it is consistently right, you're picking up data of some form. If not, you _probably_ are not (there are some weird inverted results which are statistically significant --and can even be useful-- but give results far _worse_ than can be attributed to pure chance).

88% is quite good, really, over the long term. You don't indicate how many times you've played, though.

The short answer, though, is that you generally _don't_ know whether "what you're receiving" is the real thing, or imagined. That's a large part of the reason for the protocols in Remote Viewing, for example: to have a way to weed out some of the imagined "data".
