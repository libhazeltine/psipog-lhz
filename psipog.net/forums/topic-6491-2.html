I don't know anyone who is knowledgeable enough to lecture on Ninjutsu except for Soke Hatsumi and/or Stephen Hayes.

Ninjutsu used to be my passion and I researched it's history for fun. The sensing of the intent of someone who is willing to harm you is called "Force of the Killer". Students are often tested by sitting down while the teacher takes a wooden sword and holds it on top of his head in a ready thrusting position. At any point the teacher must maintain a constant desire to kill the student or the student has nothing to sense. Whenever the teacher desires he may thrust the sword down and hopefully the student sense and reacts quickly enough. It may seem like a ridiculous exercise because serious injury can occur but then the teacher will say that the real thing is required because that is what you are learning in Ninjutsu.

As for levitating and flying etc. It was doutable that active ninja would employ such a tactic because a majority of their study and practice was devoted to stealthily entering or perhaps faking identities to enter by the most simple means. A lot of the activity of the ninja were purely psychological such as maintaining positions and plans according to 5 elements and leaking information etc. But still there were several mystic arts attributed to the ninja, perhaps to the yamabushi and the sohei (mountain monks and warrior monks whom several stories and rumors circulated about their supernatural powers). The warrior classes of Japan are basically a very confusing study.

What you have here is basically a rumor. Since the Ninja and Yamabushi were shrouded in mystery, mysticism and ignorance people were led to believe they could levitate, walk on water, transform into animals etc. Where in reality they were using handgliders, special boots to trasverse rivers and when people followed the footprints of a ninja they might seem to go both ways at once but this was only because he used a special walking technique or they might seem to turn into tiger's footprints but this was only because he domesticated a tiger at a certain point would ride it for the rest of the ride. So you see, samurai, and peasants made up such wild stories for amusement while samurai did so to escape punishment because who could kill a person that could transform into a tiger.

So basically what you have is just another rumor. Some rumors were reinforced by the fact that the sole dedication of a yambushi or shugenja was to develop spiritual powers through ascetic practice.

I must apologize to anyone here who may be intent in their falsehoods before beginning...

Neveza is correct. The ninja, shinoubi, are the assassin warrior class of feudal Japan. They were much like secret agents in the movies and could basically be characterized as a wolf in sheep's clothing type of group. They most definitely were skilled killers and were very stealthy, but their public image was totally hyped for the purpose of gaining a notoriety that might as well be mystical.
As for the technique, that is also correct, to a point; it is called hing kung in cantonese, qing gong in mandarin, and it means the lightness skill/art of lightness. It contrasts with yin gong (art of hardness- you may have seen the video on TV of the chinese master being stabbed with a sword that just bends when it hits him. This is yin gong), and is developed entirely through physical dilligence.
I see no connection between that technique and the ninja, who did use a similar technique borrowed from shaolin kung fu, but it was not mystical either. The training simply constisted of jumping with weights on out of a pit whose depth was increased regularly, along with the weight. This performed the function of increasing their unweighted jumping height, which was useful in the built-up architecture of Japan.
There is little esoteric content to the true ninja practice, although challenging their public image is almost insulting to the Japanese culture.
