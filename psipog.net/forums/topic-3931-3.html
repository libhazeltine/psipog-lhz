Apollo wrote:
wait, so every billionth time a ball is dropped, it will hover? I disagree. I think you either mis-understood your professor or he mis-spoke. I garrentee to you that gravity works 100% of the time.


I think a lot of people have a misconception of what the scientific theory is. It's not about proving theories, it's about disproving theories. A good scientist will construct a hypothesis surrounding an experiment and then will test that theory until it is disproven (or fails to be). If at any moment that hypothetical ball did hover upon being dropped, then the theory of gravity would have to be scrapped and a new one would take its place until that is disproven. Ergo, there is always the possibility that one day gravity may very well fail us as we know it.

People don't like not having absolutes to go by so it's easy to think of science as the only way to knowledge. But on the contrary - it's much better off at pointing out what we don't know.
