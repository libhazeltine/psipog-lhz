UltimaRage wrote:
If subliminal messaging had no credability, than why is it against the law to use it coupled with any other advertising? Sounds outrageous, but true.


If I'm not mistaken, the Wikipedia entry gives a clear and concise answer to this question"

Quote:
Vicary's claims led to a public outcry, and to many conspiracy theories of governments and cults using the technique to their advantage. The practice of subliminal advertising was subsequently banned in the United Kingdom, Australia[1] and in the United States (by the National Association of Broadcasters in 1958, and under the law, in 1974, by the Federal Communications Commission (FCC)[3])


People freaked out, then government freaked out, and now it's illegal. This doesn't mean there's a conspiracy.

I've done research into subliminal messaging for a cognitive psych research report I wrote a few years ago. Trust me, subliminal stimulus isn't received like it's comically portrayed. Flashing messages doesn't make you smoke or buy products. What it has been shown to do, in some studies, is affect a person's mood, or prime them in such a way that they make a predictable value judgment after they're shown the subliminal stimulus.

However, the research is, at best, ambivalent, and certainly far from showing subliminal messaging as an effective marketing technique.
