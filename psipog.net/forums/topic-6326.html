I hadn't really thought about spoon-bending until recently, when we covered metallic bonds in chemistry. For those who don't know, here's an review:

A metallic bond is essentially, as the name suggests, a bond between several metal atoms (e.g. iron, sodium, magnesium, etc.). This bond is formed by the metal atoms losing electrons (becoming ions), and the electrons forming a "sea" of delocalized electrons. This is what makes metals conductive and ductile; when a chunk of metal is bent, the "sea" of electrons is what is really being bent, not the atoms themselves, and flowing electricity is the delocalized electrons flowing through their parent material. These bonds can be tricky to break, which is why metals are (generally) so hard to break or melt. The more electrons the bond has, the stronger the bond.

Now that you have reviewed the basics, here's my theory:

Perhaps when a metal spoon is bent, the delocalized electrons are really being removed from the bond so that it weakens. This allows the metal in the spoon to become more ductile, allowing the person attempting to bend the spoon to bend it much more easily either with their hands or with PK.

One way to test it: perform the spoon-bending trick, actually reaching the stage where the metal is bent, then attempting to melt the spoon. If it melts at a lower temperature than the spoon normally would have, then the theory is correct.

If this is true, then spoon-bending is actually a form of electro-PK.

Anyone with a blowtorch up to test it?
