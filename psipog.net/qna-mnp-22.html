Peebrain replies on June 9, 2004, 7:38pm:

Alrighty - first thing I'll address is a small technicallity: when you make a psi ball visible, you are flaring it. You can also accidently flare a psi ball. So it's not "accident or flaring", it's "accidental flaring or intentional flaring". But now to the heart of your question...

This is what's going on - when people talk about accidental flaring, they are usually beginners who don't know how to flare intentionally. They usually make a psi ball, like they always do, but since they have a bunch of practice under their belt, they may put some extra oomph into the current one they're making. As a result, it might flare. So, at first, it's usually accidental and it does take focus and practice to learn how to do it intentionally.

After you get the hang of it after a while, you can intentionally flare a psi ball "so simple". So, when you read an article about someone flaring it easily, that's because they already went through that stages of messing around with it, and having a hard time getting it to flare. They practice, they learn, and NOW it comes to them easily. When you read about someone accidentally flaring, they are still in the "earlier stages", and at that time, it is harder.

TheFlameOfGod asks on May 9, 2004, 2:06pm:

What is the difference between a psi-ball and a ki-ball? The visualization process seems very similar, and the only difference that I have seen in abilities is that the ki-ball (within-reason) can heal. How can I tell the difference if I have made a ki-ball or a psi-ball? Thank you for taking the time. God bless.

Rainsong replies on May 9, 2004, 4:38pm:

A "psi-ball" is made out of "psi"; a "ki-ball" is made out of "ki". (Likewise, "tinfoil-balls" are made out of "tinfoil"; "icecream-balls" are made out of icecream; "lint-balls" are made out of "lint"; "snow-balls" are made out of "snow";....you get the idea.)

If a "ki-ball" can accomplish any verifiable effect in its own right -- be it healing, glowing, or knocking over your kid-sister's glass of orange juice-- it is therefore _also_ a "psi-ball". "Psi" is a fairly broad term, defined more by what it can _do_ rather than what it _is_.
