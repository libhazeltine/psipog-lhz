Rainsong replies on November 16, 2003, 4:54pm:

Excellent question. With some targets of psychokinetic activity, there is varying degrees of probability that the intended effect could result from some other factor at that time. The psiwheel is a notorious example: heat from the hands, and assorted small breezes can move it. A pencil moving around the table, in a roughly circular path, is less likely but still possible (combination of an odd warp in the table top and a lot of heavy trucks or a train passing nearby, perhaps?) Some other experimental targets (such as repeatedly stopping a radiometer without interfering with the light source) are much more difficult to find an alternative cause for. Likewise, causing wave patterns in cloud chambers. If there is no known way to make an effect happen other than PK, as in the cases of the radiometer and the cloud chamber, then the result may be ascribed tentatively to PK, until such a time in the future when some other mechanism might be discovered to cause those specific effects.

Rainsong replies on November 6, 2003, 10:16pm:

Potassium and glucose, actually...

The most easily obtained references for this are still pretty obscure. Bevy Jaegers makes reference to it in her book _The Psychic Paradigm_. There are some comments regarding the effects of pk on blood chemistry in some accounts of Nina Kulagina's experiences.

There are studies currently going on, looking into specifics of this. These two are pretty easy to check, glucose being the easier of the two. Quick blood test devices for blood glucose are made for use by diabetics. Such devices are handy for checking fluctuations in experiemental conditions, too, although the supplies for the tests can be rather expensive (and require a prescription in some places).
